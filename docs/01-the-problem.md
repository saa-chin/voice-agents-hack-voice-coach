# The Problem

## 12 million people are slowly losing their voice — and we are not helping them practice

Parkinson's disease affects an estimated **10 million people worldwide**, and another **2 million** live with post-stroke dysarthria, ALS, MS, or cerebral palsy that impair the muscles of speech. **Up to 90% of people with Parkinson's** develop a speech disorder called *hypokinetic dysarthria*: their voice becomes quiet, monotone, breathy, and rushed. Strangers ask them to repeat themselves. Family members start finishing their sentences. Many stop talking on the phone. Many stop talking at dinner. Many stop talking.

This is not a cosmetic problem. Loss of voice is one of the strongest predictors of social withdrawal, depression, and accelerated cognitive decline in Parkinson's patients.

## The therapy works. The access does not.

There is a clinically proven treatment. **LSVT LOUD** and **SPEAK OUT!** are evidence-based speech therapy protocols that retrain patients to speak with appropriate loudness and intent. Multiple randomized controlled trials show measurable, lasting improvement.

The problem is the delivery model:

- **The standard protocol is 16 in-person sessions over 4 weeks**, followed by daily home practice for life.
- There are roughly **150,000 speech-language pathologists in the US** for tens of millions of patients with communication disorders. Wait times of 2 to 6 months are normal.
- A single session costs **$150 to $300** and is often not covered by insurance.
- After the 16 sessions end, the patient is sent home with a paper booklet of exercises and is expected to practice every day, alone, with no feedback, for the rest of their life.

**Adherence collapses.** Studies of post-discharge home-practice compliance in dysarthria patients show drop-off rates of 60 to 80% within the first three months. The therapy works in the clinic and then evaporates at home.

## Why the existing tech does not solve this

A patient looking for help between sessions has three bad options:

| Option | Why it fails |
| --- | --- |
| Voice memos + self-review | Patients with hypophonia cannot hear that they are quiet — that is the disorder itself. Self-review is the blind judging the blind. |
| Generic voice assistants (Siri, Alexa) | Built to recognize healthy speech. They fail on dysarthric voices, then the patient gives up, embarrassed. |
| Cloud-based speech apps | Voice is biometric PHI. Streaming a vulnerable patient's struggling speech to a third-party server raises consent, HIPAA, and dignity concerns that have kept clinicians from recommending them. |
| Existing speech-therapy apps | Most are glorified flashcards. None measure prosody (loudness, pitch, pace) in real time. None use multimodal AI. None work offline. |

## What patients and clinicians actually need

We spoke to the literature, to caregivers, and to the published guidelines from LSVT Global and the Parkinson Voice Project. The unmet need is consistent:

1. **Real-time, objective feedback on loudness, pitch range, and pace** — the things the patient cannot self-assess.
2. **A daily practice loop short enough to actually do** — 5 to 10 minutes, not an hour.
3. **Privacy by default** — the recording of a person struggling to speak should never leave their device unless they explicitly choose to share it.
4. **Works anywhere, anytime** — in the car, on a plane, in a rural area with no signal, in a nursing home with locked-down wifi.
5. **A trend the patient and their clinician can both see** — so the next 30-minute appointment is spent on what is not improving, not on guessing.

## The opening

Until now, building this was not possible on a phone. Real-time multimodal voice understanding required a server. Today, with **Gemma 4** running on the **Cactus** runtime, a 4-billion-parameter multimodal model with native audio understanding fits in the patient's pocket and responds in 0.3 seconds.

The therapy is solved. The science is solved. **The delivery is what is broken — and it is now, for the first time, fixable.**
